Acute Care Nurse

Acute care nurses work in fast-paced settings such as emergency departments, intensive care units, and surgical recovery rooms. They provide immediate treatment for patients with urgent, severe, or rapidly changing conditions. These nurses must be skilled in clinical assessment and quick decision-making. Acute care nurses typically hold a BSN and may obtain certification in critical care. Salaries range from $75,000 to $95,000 depending on location and experience.

Read more about Acute Care Nursing

Acute Care Nurse Practitioner

Acute care nurse practitioners (ACNPs) are advanced practice nurses who diagnose and manage acute and complex health conditions in adults and older adults. Working in settings such as hospitals, trauma centers, and specialty clinics, ACNPs perform procedures, order tests, prescribe medications, and coordinate care. Most hold a DNP or MSN in acute care. Average salaries range from $105,000 to $130,000 annually.

Read more about Acute Care Nurse Practitioners

Addiction Nurse

Addiction nurses care for individuals struggling with substance use disorders. They work in rehab centers, detox units, outpatient clinics, and community programs. Their responsibilities include medication management, counseling support, and monitoring withdrawal symptoms. Many pursue the Certified Addiction Registered Nurse (CARN) credential. Addiction nurses typically earn between $70,000 and $90,000 annually, depending on experience and setting.

Read more about Addiction Nursing

Adult Gerontology Acute Care Nurse Practitioner

Adult gerontology acute care nurse practitioners (AGACNPs) specialize in caring for acutely ill adults and older adults. They manage chronic and complex conditions in hospital settings, intensive care units, and specialty clinics. AGACNPs perform procedures, interpret diagnostic tests, and develop treatment plans. This role requires a graduate degree and national certification. Salaries typically range from $110,000 to $135,000 per year.

Read more about Adult Gerontology Acute Care Nurse Practitioners

Adult Gerontology Nurse Practitioner

Adult gerontology nurse practitioners (AGNPs) provide primary and preventive care for patients from adolescence through older adulthood. They assess, diagnose, treat, and manage a wide range of health conditions, often in outpatient or long-term care settings. AGNPs typically hold an MSN or DNP and are board certified. The average salary for AGNPs is between $100,000 and $120,000 annually.

Read more about Adult Gerontology Nurse Practitioners

Adult Nurse Practitioner

Adult nurse practitioners (ANPs) focus on the care of patients ages 13 and older, managing acute and chronic conditions across adult populations. They work in private practices, clinics, and hospitals, emphasizing health promotion and disease prevention. ANPs must complete an MSN or DNP program and obtain certification. Salaries typically range from $95,000 to $115,000 per year.

Read more about Adult Nurse Practitioner

Advanced Practice Nurse

Advanced practice nurses (APNs) are registered nurses who have earned at least a master’s degree in a nursing specialty. This group includes nurse practitioners, nurse anesthetists, clinical nurse specialists, and nurse midwives. APNs deliver high-level care, often diagnosing, treating, and managing patient health independently. Salaries vary widely by role, but most earn between $100,000 and $150,000 annually.

Read more about Advanced Practice Nurses

Aesthetic Nurse

Aesthetic nurses, also called cosmetic nurses, specialize in non-surgical procedures such as Botox, fillers, laser treatments, and chemical peels. They work in dermatology clinics, medical spas, or plastic surgery offices. These RNs must be skilled in patient assessment and aesthetics techniques. Aesthetic nurses typically earn between $80,000 and $105,000 annually, depending on services and location.

Read more about Aesthetic Nurses

Aesthetic Nurse Practitioner

Aesthetic nurse practitioners provide advanced aesthetic care, including injectables, laser therapies, and anti-aging treatments. With prescriptive authority and autonomy, they often operate independently or in collaboration with physicians. ANPs must complete an MSN or DNP and may hold certifications in aesthetic medicine. Salaries range from $100,000 to $140,000 annually, with potential for higher earnings in private practice.

Read more about Aesthetic Nurse Practitioners

Agency Nurse

Agency nurses are employed through staffing agencies and work in a variety of clinical settings on temporary assignments. Their flexibility allows them to fill in during staffing shortages or seasonal surges. Agency nurses must be quick learners and adaptable. Compensation is often higher than traditional roles, with hourly rates leading to annual salaries of $90,000 to $120,000 depending on location and contract terms.

Read more about Agency Nursing

Allergy Nurse

Allergy nurses specialize in caring for patients with allergies, asthma, and other immune-related conditions. They assist in administering allergy tests, giving immunotherapy injections, educating patients on allergen avoidance, and managing treatment plans. These nurses typically work in outpatient clinics, ENT practices, or specialty allergy centers. With strong knowledge in immunology and patient education, they play a vital role in long-term allergy management. Allergy nurses generally earn between $70,000 and $90,000 per year.

Read more about Allergy Nurses

Ambulatory Care Nurse

Ambulatory care nurses provide care to patients in outpatient settings, including clinics, same-day surgery centers, and private practices. They manage chronic illnesses, triage patients, perform wound care, administer medications, and offer patient education. These nurses need strong assessment and communication skills to coordinate care in a fast-paced, high-volume environment. Salaries typically range from $75,000 to $95,000 annually.

Read more about Ambulatory Care Nurses

Burn ICU Nurse

Burn ICU nurses care for patients with severe burns requiring intensive treatment. These nurses manage complex wound care, fluid resuscitation, pain control, and infection prevention. Working in burn centers and trauma hospitals, they collaborate with surgeons and rehab teams to promote recovery. Due to the high acuity and emotional demands, burn ICU nurses must possess advanced clinical skills and resilience. Salaries range from $85,000 to $105,000 per year.

Read more about Burn ICU Nursing

Camp Nurse

Camp nurses work in summer camps, retreat centers, and other outdoor programs to care for campers and staff. They treat minor injuries and illnesses, administer medications, monitor chronic conditions, and ensure infection control protocols. These nurses must be self-sufficient and prepared for a wide variety of medical needs in remote environments. Camp nurses often earn seasonal pay ranging from $1,000 to $2,000 per week.

Read more about Camp Nursing

Cardiac Nurse

Cardiac nurses specialize in caring for patients with heart conditions such as heart failure, arrhythmias, and post-cardiac surgery recovery. They work in telemetry units, cardiac catheterization labs, and cardiac rehab centers. Responsibilities include monitoring cardiac rhythms, administering cardiac medications, and providing lifestyle education. Salaries typically range from $80,000 to $100,000 annually depending on experience and setting.

Read more about Cardiac Nursing

Care Transition Coordinator Nurse

Care transition coordinator nurses help patients navigate their healthcare journey from hospital to home or another care setting. They manage discharge planning, ensure medication reconciliation, schedule follow-up care, and educate patients and families to reduce readmissions. These nurses are skilled in communication and care coordination. Salaries range from $75,000 to $95,000 annually.

Read more about Care Transition Coordinator Nursing

Case Management Nurse

Case management nurses oversee patient care plans across the healthcare continuum. They assess needs, coordinate services, advocate for resources, and ensure cost-effective, quality care. These nurses often work for hospitals, insurance companies, or government agencies. Certification as a Certified Case Manager (CCM) is common. Salaries range from $80,000 to $100,000 annually.

Read more about Case Management Nursing

Cath Lab Nurse

Cath lab nurses assist with cardiac catheterizations and other interventional cardiovascular procedures. They prepare patients, monitor vital signs, and support the cardiologist during procedures. Strong knowledge of cardiovascular anatomy, hemodynamics, and emergency protocols is essential. Cath lab nurses often work in hospitals and heart centers, earning between $90,000 and $110,000 annually.

Read more about Cath Lab Nursing

CCU Nurse

CCU (Coronary Care Unit) nurses provide critical care to patients with severe cardiac conditions such as heart attacks, post-op cardiac surgery recovery, and cardiac arrest. They are trained in advanced life support and continuous cardiac monitoring. CCU nurses often collaborate closely with cardiologists and emergency teams. Salaries typically fall between $85,000 and $105,000 per year.

Read more about CCU Nursing

Certified Nurse Midwife

Certified nurse midwives (CNMs) provide prenatal, labor and delivery, and postpartum care, as well as general women’s health services. They are advanced practice nurses who often work in hospitals, birthing centers, and private practices. CNMs may deliver babies, prescribe medications, and provide contraceptive and menopausal care. With an MSN or DNP and board certification, CNMs typically earn between $105,000 and $130,000 annually.

Read more about Certified Nurse Midwifes

Certified Registered Nurse Anesthetist

Certified registered nurse anesthetists (CRNAs) are advanced practice nurses who administer anesthesia for surgeries, labor and delivery, and pain management procedures. They work in hospitals, surgical centers, dental clinics, and independent practices. CRNAs assess patients, deliver anesthesia, and monitor vital signs throughout procedures. This high-responsibility role requires a doctorate-level education (DNAP or DNP) and national certification. CRNAs earn some of the highest nursing salaries, typically ranging from $180,000 to $220,000 annually.

Read more about Certified Registered Nurse Anesthetists

Charge Nurse

Charge nurses oversee the operations of a nursing unit during their shift. They manage staff assignments, coordinate patient care, troubleshoot clinical issues, and serve as the point of contact for administration. Charge nurses must balance leadership duties with direct patient care and often act as mentors to newer nurses. Salaries range from $80,000 to $100,000 depending on experience and facility type.

Read more about Charge Nurses

Chief Nursing Officer

Chief nursing officers (CNOs) are senior executives responsible for the overall leadership of nursing services within a healthcare organization. They develop policies, oversee budgets, ensure compliance, and guide strategic initiatives. CNOs influence care delivery and workforce management at the highest levels. Most hold a DNP or MHA and have extensive leadership experience. Salaries range from $140,000 to $250,000 or more.

Read more about Chief Nursing Officers

Clinic Nurse

Clinic nurses work in outpatient medical settings, including primary care clinics, specialty practices, and urgent care centers. They assist with exams, administer medications and vaccines, triage patients, and provide health education. Clinic nurses are essential for ongoing patient management and chronic care coordination. Salaries typically range from $70,000 to $90,000 annually.

Read more about Clinic Nursing

Clinical Nurse Auditor

Clinical nurse auditors review medical records to ensure accuracy, regulatory compliance, and proper billing. They identify coding discrepancies, support risk management, and help improve documentation practices. These nurses work for hospitals, insurance companies, and government agencies. A background in coding and informatics is often helpful. Salaries range from $80,000 to $100,000 annually.

Read more about Clinical Nurse Auditors

Clinical Nurse Educator

Clinical nurse educators teach and mentor nurses in academic or clinical settings. They design curriculum, conduct in-service training, and guide evidence-based practice implementation. Many work in hospitals or nursing schools, fostering professional development and improving patient outcomes. Most hold a master’s or doctoral degree in nursing education. Salaries typically range from $85,000 to $110,000.

Read more about Clinical Nurse Educators

Clinical Nurse Leader

Clinical nurse leaders (CNLs) are advanced generalist clinicians who focus on improving care quality and patient safety across units. They coordinate team-based care, analyze outcomes, and implement evidence-based practices. CNLs require a specialized MSN and national certification. They often work in acute care or complex clinical environments. Salaries generally range from $90,000 to $115,000 per year.

Read more about Clinical Nurse Leaders

Clinical Nurse Specialist

Clinical nurse specialists (CNSs) are advanced practice nurses who provide expert care in a specialized area such as oncology, cardiology, or mental health. They serve as clinical experts, educators, researchers, and consultants. CNSs hold a master’s or doctoral degree and national certification in their specialty. Salaries typically range from $95,000 to $120,000 annually.

Read more about Clinical Nurse Specialists

Clinical Trial Nurse

Clinical trial nurses coordinate and manage research studies involving new treatments or interventions. They recruit participants, administer investigational drugs, collect data, and ensure regulatory compliance. These nurses bridge the gap between research and clinical care, contributing to medical advancements. Salaries range from $85,000 to $105,000, with higher earning potential in industry-sponsored trials.

Read more about Clinical Trial Nursing

Community Health Nurse

Community health nurses work with populations in schools, shelters, public health departments, and outreach clinics. Their focus is on prevention, education, and improving access to care. They often conduct screenings, immunizations, home visits, and health education programs. Community health nurses play a vital role in reducing health disparities. Salaries typically range from $70,000 to $90,000 per year.

Read more about Community Health Nursing

Cosmetic Nurse

Cosmetic nurses, also known as aesthetic nurses, provide non-surgical cosmetic treatments such as Botox, dermal fillers, chemical peels, and laser therapy. They work in dermatology clinics, med spas, or plastic surgery practices. These nurses must have a strong understanding of facial anatomy, aesthetics, and patient communication. Many obtain specialized certifications in cosmetic procedures. Salaries typically range from $80,000 to $105,000 annually.

Read more about Cosmetic Nursing

CVICU Nurse

CVICU (Cardiovascular Intensive Care Unit) nurses care for critically ill patients recovering from heart surgery, heart attacks, or severe cardiac conditions. They monitor vital signs, administer vasoactive medications, and manage ventilators and other life-sustaining technologies. These nurses work closely with cardiologists and surgeons. Salaries generally range from $90,000 to $115,000 per year depending on experience.

Read more about CVICU Nursing

Dental Nurse

Dental nurses assist dentists during procedures, manage instruments, maintain a sterile field, and provide patient education. They may also perform administrative duties and support infection control protocols. Dental nurses often work in dental offices and oral surgery centers. While this role is more common in the UK and other countries, U.S. equivalents may be dental assistants with nursing backgrounds. Salaries typically range from $50,000 to $70,000 annually.

Read more about Dental Nursing

Dermatology Nurse

Dermatology nurses care for patients with skin conditions such as eczema, acne, psoriasis, and skin cancer. They assist with biopsies, wound care, phototherapy, and patient education. These nurses often work in dermatology offices and outpatient surgical centers. A certification in dermatology nursing may be pursued. Salaries usually range from $75,000 to $95,000 per year.

Read more about Dermatology Nursing

Dermatology Nurse Practitioner

Dermatology nurse practitioners (DNPs) are advanced practice nurses who diagnose and treat skin conditions, perform biopsies and minor procedures, prescribe medications, and manage long-term dermatologic care. They often work autonomously in clinics or in collaboration with dermatologists. With specialized training and certifications, DNPs can earn between $110,000 and $140,000 annually.

Read more about Dermatology Nurse Practitioners

Developmental Disabilities Nurse

Developmental disabilities nurses provide care to individuals with intellectual and developmental disorders such as Down syndrome, autism, or cerebral palsy. They work in schools, group homes, residential facilities, and community health settings. These nurses focus on promoting independence, communication, and overall quality of life. Salaries typically range from $70,000 to $90,000 per year.

Read more about Developmental Disabilities Nursing

Diabetes Nurse

Diabetes nurses specialize in helping patients manage type 1, type 2, and gestational diabetes. They monitor blood sugar levels, provide medication education, and support lifestyle changes to prevent complications. These nurses work in hospitals, clinics, and community settings. Many pursue certification as diabetes care and education specialists (CDCES). Salaries range from $75,000 to $95,000 annually.

Read more about Diabetes Nursing

Diabetes Nurse Educator

Diabetes nurse educators provide in-depth education to patients and families on managing diabetes through diet, exercise, medication, and glucose monitoring. They work in hospitals, endocrinology clinics, and community health centers. These nurses often hold certification as CDCES (Certified Diabetes Care and Education Specialist). Salaries typically range from $80,000 to $100,000 per year.

Read more about Diabetes Nurse Educators

Dialysis Nurse

Dialysis nurses, also called nephrology nurses, care for patients with kidney failure undergoing hemodialysis or peritoneal dialysis. They monitor dialysis machines, assess for complications, educate patients on fluid and diet restrictions, and coordinate with nephrologists. These nurses work in outpatient dialysis centers and hospitals. Salaries range from $80,000 to $100,000 annually.

Read more about Dialysis Nursing

Discharge Nurse

Discharge nurses ensure that patients leaving the hospital have a clear understanding of their care plans, medications, follow-up appointments, and warning signs. Their goal is to improve outcomes and reduce hospital readmissions. These nurses work closely with case managers and care teams. Salaries typically range from $75,000 to $95,000 annually.

Read more about Discharge Nursing

Emergency Nurse Practitioner

Emergency nurse practitioners (ENPs) are advanced practice nurses who diagnose and treat patients in emergency departments, urgent care centers, and trauma units. They perform procedures, order diagnostic tests, prescribe medications, and manage acute illnesses or injuries. ENPs must hold a master’s or doctoral degree in emergency care and be board-certified. Salaries typically range from $115,000 to $140,000 annually.

Read more about Emergency Nurse Practitioners

Emergency Room Registered Nurse

Emergency room registered nurses (ER RNs) care for patients with a wide range of urgent and life-threatening conditions. They triage, stabilize, monitor, and treat individuals of all ages in fast-paced emergency settings. ER nurses must be skilled in critical thinking, trauma response, and rapid assessment. Salaries generally range from $80,000 to $100,000 annually.

Read more about Emergency Room Nursing

Endocrinology Nurse Practitioner

Endocrinology nurse practitioners specialize in diagnosing and managing hormonal and metabolic disorders, such as diabetes, thyroid dysfunction, and adrenal conditions. They collaborate with endocrinologists or work independently in outpatient clinics. These NPs monitor lab values, prescribe medications, and educate patients. Salaries typically range from $105,000 to $125,000 annually.

Read more about Endocrinology Nurse Practitioners

Endoscopy Nurse

Endoscopy nurses assist with procedures that examine the digestive tract, such as colonoscopies and upper endoscopies. They prepare patients, monitor sedation, and ensure safety during and after procedures. These nurses work in hospitals, surgical centers, and GI clinics. Salaries typically range from $80,000 to $95,000 per year.

Read more about Endoscopy Nursing

Environmental Health Nurse

Environmental health nurses focus on how environmental factors—such as air quality, water safety, and chemical exposure—impact public health. They work in public health agencies, government departments, and advocacy organizations. These nurses educate communities, conduct environmental assessments, and participate in policy development. Salaries range from $75,000 to $95,000 annually.

Read more about Environmental Health Nursing

Faculty Nurse

Faculty nurses are educators who teach future nurses in academic settings, including nursing schools, universities, and technical programs. They design curricula, lead lectures and labs, supervise clinical rotations, and contribute to academic research. Most hold a master’s or doctoral degree in nursing education. Salaries typically range from $80,000 to $105,000 annually.

Read more about Faculty Nursing

Faith Community Nurse

Faith community nurses, also known as parish nurses, provide spiritual and holistic care within faith-based settings. They offer health education, counseling, support for chronic conditions, and promote wellness within congregations. These nurses often serve as liaisons between the church and healthcare system. Salaries vary widely but typically fall between $65,000 and $85,000 per year.

Read more about Faith Community Nursing

Flight Nurse

Flight nurses provide critical care to patients during air medical transport via helicopter or fixed-wing aircraft. They manage trauma, cardiac events, and critical illness while airborne, often in high-pressure environments. These nurses must have advanced certifications such as CCRN or CFRN. Salaries generally range from $90,000 to $120,000 annually.

Read more about Flight Nursing

Float Pool Nurse

Float pool nurses are versatile professionals who work across multiple units within a hospital or health system. They fill staffing gaps and must adapt quickly to different specialties such as med-surg, ICU, or ER. Float pool nurses often receive higher pay for their flexibility, typically earning between $85,000 and $110,000 per year.

Read more about Float Pool Nurses

Forensic Nurse

Forensic nurses care for victims of trauma, abuse, and violence, collecting evidence and providing medical testimony in legal cases. They work in emergency departments, sexual assault response teams, and correctional facilities. Many hold certification as Sexual Assault Nurse Examiners (SANE). Salaries range from $85,000 to $105,000 annually.

Read more about Forensic Nursing

Gastroenterology Nurse

Gastroenterology nurses care for patients with digestive disorders such as Crohn’s disease, ulcerative colitis, GERD, and liver disease. They assist in diagnostic procedures like colonoscopies and endoscopies, provide patient education, and manage chronic symptoms. These nurses work in outpatient clinics, GI labs, and hospitals. Salaries typically range from $80,000 to $95,000 annually.

Read more about Gastroenterology Nursing

Genetics Nurse

Genetics nurses work with individuals and families at risk for inherited conditions. They assist with genetic testing, provide counseling support, and collaborate with specialists to develop care plans. These nurses often work in specialty clinics, research institutions, and cancer centers. Many hold certification in genetics nursing. Salaries usually range from $80,000 to $100,000 per year.

Read more about Genetics Nurses

Geriatric Nurse

Geriatric nurses specialize in the care of older adults, focusing on managing chronic conditions, mobility, cognitive changes, and overall wellness. They work in long-term care facilities, hospitals, home health, and assisted living settings. These nurses are skilled in fall prevention, medication management, and family support. Salaries typically range from $75,000 to $95,000 per year.

Read more about Geriatric Nurses

Global Health Nurse

Global health nurses work internationally or with underserved populations to improve health outcomes through public health initiatives, disease prevention, and health education. They collaborate with NGOs, humanitarian groups, and global agencies. These nurses must be adaptable and culturally sensitive. Salaries vary widely but often range from $70,000 to $100,000 depending on the organization and role.

Read more about Global Health Nursing

Government Nurse

Government nurses are employed by federal, state, or local agencies, including the VA, public health departments, military branches, and correctional systems. They provide direct care, conduct inspections, or manage public health initiatives. Roles vary by agency, and salaries typically range from $80,000 to $110,000 depending on level and location.

Read more about Government Nurses

Hematology Nurse

Hematology nurses specialize in caring for patients with blood disorders such as anemia, leukemia, lymphoma, and clotting conditions. They administer chemotherapy, manage transfusions, and support patients undergoing bone marrow transplants. These nurses work in cancer centers and hematology clinics. Salaries range from $85,000 to $105,000 annually.

Read more about Hematology Nursing

Holistic Nurse

Holistic nurses integrate complementary therapies—such as massage, aromatherapy, meditation, and nutrition—into traditional nursing care to promote healing of the mind, body, and spirit. They often work in integrative health centers, hospice, or private practice. Certification as a holistic nurse is available. Salaries typically range from $75,000 to $95,000 per year.

Read more about Holistic Nursing

Home Health Nurse

Home health nurses provide skilled nursing care in patients’ homes, including wound care, medication administration, chronic disease management, and health monitoring. They work independently, traveling between homes to deliver care and education. Flexibility and strong assessment skills are crucial. Salaries generally range from $80,000 to $100,000 annually.

Read more about Home Health Nursing

Home Hospice Nurse

Home hospice nurses deliver compassionate end-of-life care in patients’ homes. They manage pain and symptoms, provide emotional support, and educate families on the dying process. These nurses work closely with interdisciplinary teams and must be skilled in communication and comfort care. Salaries typically range from $80,000 to $95,000 per year.

Read more about Home Hospice Nursing

Hospice Nurse

Hospice nurses care for terminally ill patients in hospice centers, nursing homes, or private residences. They focus on palliative care, managing pain, providing emotional support, and improving quality of life during the final stages. Hospice nurses often work in teams alongside social workers and chaplains. Salaries range from $80,000 to $100,000 annually.

Read more about Hospice Nursing

Hospitalist Nurse Practitioner

Hospitalist nurse practitioners (NPs) manage the care of hospitalized patients from admission to discharge. They assess patients, order and interpret tests, coordinate with specialists, and develop treatment plans. Hospitalist NPs typically work in internal medicine or acute care settings and often cover multiple units. Salaries typically range from $110,000 to $130,000 per year.

Read more about Hospitalist Nurse Practitioners

ICU Nurse

ICU (Intensive Care Unit) nurses care for critically ill patients who require continuous monitoring and complex interventions. They manage ventilators, titrate life-saving medications, and provide support to families during medical crises. ICU nurses must possess advanced critical thinking and clinical skills. Salaries generally range from $90,000 to $110,000 annually.

Read more about ICU Nurses

Immunology Nurse

Immunology nurses treat patients with immune system disorders such as lupus, HIV, multiple sclerosis, and immunodeficiency syndromes. They may administer biologic therapies, monitor for reactions, and educate patients about managing chronic conditions. These nurses often work in specialty clinics and research settings. Salaries range from $80,000 to $100,000 annually.

Read more about Immunology Nurses

Infection Control Nurse

Infection control nurses develop and implement strategies to prevent and manage infections in healthcare settings. They track infection rates, train staff on hygiene practices, and ensure compliance with public health regulations. These nurses play a vital role in reducing hospital-acquired infections. Salaries typically range from $85,000 to $105,000 per year.

Read more about Infection Control Nursing

Infectious Disease Nurse

Infectious disease nurses specialize in the care of patients with contagious illnesses such as tuberculosis, MRSA, HIV, or COVID-19. They assist in diagnosing infections, administer antiviral or antibiotic treatments, and educate patients on preventing transmission. These nurses often work in hospitals, clinics, and public health departments. Salaries range from $85,000 to $105,000 annually.

This role helps reduce unnecessary treatments and control healthcare costs. Salaries generally range from $85,000 to $105,000 per year.

Vascular Access Nurse

Vascular access nurses specialize in inserting and managing IV lines, PICC lines, and central venous catheters. They ensure safe and effective access for medication delivery and nutrition. These nurses work in hospitals, infusion centers, and home health. Salaries typically range from $85,000 to $105,000 annually.

Victim Advocate Nurse

Victim advocate nurses support patients who have experienced trauma, abuse, or violence. They provide compassionate care, collect forensic evidence, and collaborate with legal and social services. Many hold Sexual Assault Nurse Examiner (SANE) certification. Salaries typically range from $85,000 to $105,000 annually.

Women’s Health Nurse

Women’s health nurses provide care across the female lifespan, including reproductive health, family planning, prenatal/postpartum support, and menopause management. They work in OB/GYN clinics, hospitals, and community health settings. Salaries typically range from $80,000 to $100,000 per year.

Women’s Health Nurse Practitioner

Women’s health nurse practitioners (WHNPs) specialize in advanced care for women’s reproductive and overall health. They perform exams, prescribe contraceptives, manage pregnancy care, and treat gynecological conditions. WHNPs often work independently in clinics and hospitals. Salaries typically range from $110,000 to $130,000 annually.

Wound Ostomy Continence Nurse

Wound ostomy continence (WOC) nurses care for patients with wounds, stomas, and incontinence. They assess and treat complex wounds, teach ostomy management, and implement continence strategies. These nurses hold WOCN certification and work in hospitals, home care, and outpatient settings. Salaries typically range from $90,000 to $110,000 annually.
